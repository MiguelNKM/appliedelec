Unit 1: Resistors

1.1 Introduction to Resistors

Resistors are fundamental components in electrical circuits that oppose the flow of electric current. They are used to control voltage and current in circuits.

1.2 Types of Resistors

  • Fixed Resistors: Carbon film, Metal film, Wire wound
  • Variable Resistors: Potentiometers, Rheostats
  • Special Resistors: Thermistors, Light Dependent Resistors (LDRs)

1.3 Resistor Color Code

The resistor color code is a system used to indicate the resistance value and tolerance of a resistor. It consists of colored bands on the resistor body.

1.4 Resistors in Series and Parallel

Resistors can be connected in series, parallel, or a combination of both. The total resistance depends on the connection type:

  • Series: Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
  • Parallel: 1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3 + ...

1.5 Recent Advancements in Resistor Technology

  • Thin Film Resistors: Offer high precision and stability
  • Surface Mount Resistors: Enable miniaturization of electronic devices
  • Quantum Tunneling Composite (QTC) Resistors: Pressure-sensitive variable resistors
Unit 2: Insulators and Conductors

2.1 Conductors

Conductors are materials that allow the flow of electric current with minimal resistance. Examples include metals like copper, aluminum, and silver.

2.2 Insulators

Insulators are materials that strongly oppose the flow of electric current. Examples include rubber, glass, and plastic.

2.3 Semiconductors

Semiconductors have properties between those of conductors and insulators. Their conductivity can be controlled by doping or applying external fields. Examples include silicon and germanium.

2.4 Band Theory of Solids

The band theory explains the electrical properties of materials based on their electron energy bands:

  • Conductors: Overlapping valence and conduction bands
  • Insulators: Large band gap between valence and conduction bands
  • Semiconductors: Small band gap between valence and conduction bands

2.5 Recent Advancements

  • Topological Insulators: Materials that are insulators in their interior but conduct electricity on their surface
  • High-Temperature Superconductors: Materials that conduct electricity with zero resistance at relatively high temperatures
Unit 3: Resistivity of a Conductor

3.1 Definition of Resistivity

Resistivity (ρ) is an intrinsic property of a material that quantifies its resistance to electrical current flow. It is measured in ohm-meters (Ω⋅m).

3.2 Resistivity Formula

ρ = RA / L

Where:

  • ρ = Resistivity (Ω⋅m)
  • R = Resistance (Ω)
  • A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
  • L = Length (m)

3.3 Factors Affecting Resistivity

  • Temperature: Generally, resistivity increases with temperature for metals
  • Impurities: Increase resistivity
  • Mechanical strain: Can increase or decrease resistivity

3.4 Conductivity

Conductivity (σ) is the reciprocal of resistivity: σ = 1 / ρ

3.5 Recent Advancements

  • Nanomaterials: Exhibit unique resistivity properties at the nanoscale
  • Graphene: Extremely low resistivity and high electron mobility
Unit 4: Power and Energy

4.1 Electrical Power

Power is the rate at which energy is transferred or converted. In electrical circuits, it's the product of voltage and current.

P = VI = I²R = V² / R

Where:

  • P = Power (Watts, W)
  • V = Voltage (Volts, V)
  • I = Current (Amperes, A)
  • R = Resistance (Ohms, Ω)

4.2 Electrical Energy

Electrical energy is the energy converted from electrical power over a period of time.

E = P × t

Where:

  • E = Energy (Joules, J or Watt-hours, Wh)
  • P = Power (Watts, W)
  • t = Time (seconds, s or hours, h)

4.3 Power Dissipation in Resistors

When current flows through a resistor, electrical energy is converted to heat energy. This is known as Joule heating or resistive heating.

4.4 Energy Efficiency

Energy efficiency is the ratio of useful output energy to input energy, often expressed as a percentage.

4.5 Recent Advancements

  • Energy Harvesting: Converting small amounts of ambient energy into electrical energy
  • Wireless Power Transfer: Transmitting electrical energy without wires
  • Smart Grids: Advanced power networks that use digital technology to efficiently manage electricity distribution

Problem 1: Resistors in Series and Parallel

Question: Three resistors R1 = 10Ω, R2 = 20Ω, and R3 = 30Ω are connected in (a) series and (b) parallel. Calculate the total resistance in each case.

Solution:

  1. For series connection:
    Rtotal = R1 + R2 + R3 = 10Ω + 20Ω + 30Ω = 60Ω
  2. For parallel connection:
    1/Rtotal = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
    1/Rtotal = 1/10 + 1/20 + 1/30 = 6/60 + 3/60 + 2/60 = 11/60
    Rtotal = 60/11 ≈ 5.45Ω

Problem 2: Resistivity Calculation

Question: A wire has a resistance of 5Ω, a length of 10m, and a cross-sectional area of 2mm². Calculate its resistivity.

Solution:

  1. We use the formula: ρ = RA / L
  2. Given:
    R = 5Ω
    L = 10m
    A = 2mm² = 2 × 10⁻⁶ m²
  3. Substituting the values:
    ρ = (5Ω × 2 × 10⁻⁶ m²) / 10m
    ρ = 1 × 10⁻⁶ Ω⋅m = 1 µΩ⋅m

Problem 3: Power and Energy Calculation

Question: A 120V power supply delivers a current of 2A to a resistor for 5 minutes. Calculate (a) the power dissipated by the resistor and (b) the energy consumed.

Solution:

  1. Power calculation:
    P = VI = 120V × 2A = 240W
  2. Energy calculation:
    Time in seconds = 5 minutes × 60 s/min = 300s
    E = P × t = 240W × 300s = 72,000J = 72kJ
    Or in watt-hours: E = 240W × (5/60)h = 20Wh

1. Build a Voltage Divider Circuit

Objective: Construct a voltage divider circuit and measure the output voltage for different input voltages.

Materials needed:

  • Breadboard
  • Two resistors (e.g., 1kΩ and 2kΩ)
  • Variable DC power supply or batteries
  • Multimeter
  • Jumper wires

Steps:

  1. Connect the two resistors in series on the breadboard.
  2. Connect the power supply across the series combination.
  3. Measure the voltage across each resistor for different input voltages.
  4. Compare the measured values with the calculated values using the voltage divider formula: Vout = Vin × (R2 / (R1 + R2))

2. Investigate the Temperature Dependence of Resistance

Objective: Observe how the resistance of a conductor changes with temperature.

Materials needed:

  • Copper wire
  • Multimeter
  • Heat source (e.g., hot water bath)
  • Thermometer

Steps:

  1. Measure the resistance of the copper wire at room temperature.
  2. Heat the wire using the heat source, measuring its temperature.
  3. Measure the resistance at different temperatures.
  4. Plot a graph of resistance vs. temperature.
  5. Calculate the temperature coefficient of resistance for the copper wire.
