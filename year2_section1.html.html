Section 1: Alternating Current Circuit Theory

Alternating Current Circuit Theory

Alternating Current (AC) is an electric current that periodically reverses direction and changes its magnitude continuously with time. In contrast to DC, AC is the form in which electric power is delivered to businesses and residences.

1. Characteristics of AC

  • Frequency: The number of cycles per second, measured in Hertz (Hz)
  • Amplitude: The maximum value of voltage or current in each cycle
  • Phase: The position of the waveform relative to a reference point

2. AC Waveforms

The most common AC waveform is sinusoidal, but other forms include square, triangular, and sawtooth waves.

3. RLC Circuits

RLC circuits contain resistors (R), inductors (L), and capacitors (C). These components behave differently in AC circuits compared to DC circuits.

3.1 Resistors in AC Circuits

Resistors behave the same in both AC and DC circuits, following Ohm's Law: V = IR

3.2 Inductors in AC Circuits

Inductors oppose changes in current. In AC circuits, they introduce a phase shift between voltage and current. The inductive reactance is given by: XL = 2πfL

3.3 Capacitors in AC Circuits

Capacitors oppose changes in voltage. In AC circuits, they also introduce a phase shift. The capacitive reactance is given by: XC = 1 / (2πfC)

4. Power in AC Circuits

In AC circuits, power calculations are more complex due to the phase differences between voltage and current.

4.1 Types of Power

  • Real Power (P): Measured in watts (W), it's the power actually consumed in the circuit
  • Reactive Power (Q): Measured in volt-amperes reactive (VAR), it's the power stored in and discharged by inductive and capacitive elements
  • Apparent Power (S): Measured in volt-amperes (VA), it's the vector sum of real and reactive power

4.2 Power Factor

Power factor is the ratio of real power to apparent power. It's a measure of how efficiently electrical power is being used in a circuit.

5. Resonance in RLC Circuits

Resonance occurs in an RLC circuit when the inductive and capacitive reactances are equal. At resonance, the circuit impedance is purely resistive.

The resonant frequency is given by: fr = 1 / (2π√(LC))

Work Solutions

Problem 1: RLC Series Circuit

A series RLC circuit has R = 50 Ω, L = 0.1 H, and C = 100 μF. The applied voltage is 230 V at 50 Hz. Calculate:

  1. The inductive reactance
  2. The capacitive reactance
  3. The impedance
  4. The current in the circuit
  5. The phase angle between voltage and current

Solution:

  1. Inductive reactance:

    XL = 2πfL = 2π × 50 × 0.1 = 31.4 Ω

  2. Capacitive reactance:

    XC = 1 / (2πfC) = 1 / (2π × 50 × 100 × 10-6) = 31.8 Ω

  3. Impedance:

    Z = √(R2 + (XL - XC)2)

    Z = √(502 + (31.4 - 31.8)2) = 50.0 Ω

  4. Current:

    I = V / Z = 230 / 50.0 = 4.6 A

  5. Phase angle:

    φ = tan-1((XL - XC) / R)

    φ = tan-1((31.4 - 31.8) / 50) = -0.46°

Problem 2: Power Factor Correction

An AC circuit draws 10 kW at a power factor of 0.8 lagging. Calculate:

  1. The apparent power
  2. The reactive power
  3. The capacitance needed to improve the power factor to 0.95 lagging

Solution:

  1. Apparent power:

    S = P / cos φ = 10000 / 0.8 = 12500 VA = 12.5 kVA

  2. Reactive power:

    Q = √(S2 - P2) = √(125002 - 100002) = 7500 VAR = 7.5 kVAR

  3. Capacitance for power factor correction:

    New reactive power: Qnew = P × tan(cos-1(0.95)) = 10000 × tan(18.19°) = 3287 VAR

    Reactive power to be supplied by capacitor: QC = 7500 - 3287 = 4213 VAR

    Assuming 230V, 50Hz supply:

    C = QC / (2πfV2) = 4213 / (2π × 50 × 2302) = 254 μF
