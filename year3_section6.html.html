Year 3 Applied Electricity - Electrical Wiring

Electrical Wiring

1. Wiring

Electrical wiring is the foundation of any electrical system, providing the pathways for electrical current to flow safely and efficiently. Proper wiring is crucial for the safety, reliability, and efficiency of electrical installations.

1.1 Types of Wiring Systems

  • Cleat Wiring: An older method using porcelain cleats to support and insulate wires.
  • Casing and Capping Wiring: Uses wooden casing with a cap to enclose and protect wires.
  • Conduit Wiring: Wires are run through protective pipes or tubes, either surface-mounted or concealed.
  • Batten Wiring: Wires are run on wooden battens fixed to walls or ceilings.
  • Lead Sheathed Wiring: Cables with a protective lead sheath, suitable for damp conditions.
  • Structured Cabling: Modern method for data and voice communication wiring.

1.2 Cable Types and Selection

Choosing the right cable is essential for safety and performance:

  • PVC Insulated and Sheathed: Common for domestic and light commercial use.
  • XLPE (Cross-Linked Polyethylene): Higher temperature rating, suitable for industrial applications.
  • Mineral Insulated Copper Clad (MICC): Fire-resistant, used in critical circuits.
  • Armored Cables: For underground or exposed outdoor installations.
  • Fiber Optic Cables: For high-speed data transmission.

1.3 Recent Advancements in Wiring Technology

  • Smart Wiring Systems: Integrate with home automation and IoT devices.
  • Power Line Communication (PLC): Uses existing electrical wiring for data transmission.
  • Wireless Power Transfer: Emerging technology for cord-free power delivery.
  • Energy Harvesting Wires: Cables that can generate small amounts of electricity from ambient sources.

2. Protection

Electrical protection is vital to prevent damage to equipment and ensure safety for users. It involves devices and techniques to detect and respond to faults and overloads in electrical systems.

2.1 Overcurrent Protection

  • Fuses: Sacrificial devices that melt when excessive current flows.
  • Circuit Breakers: Reusable devices that trip to interrupt excessive current flow.
  • Miniature Circuit Breakers (MCBs): Combine overcurrent and short-circuit protection.

2.2 Earth Fault Protection

  • Residual Current Devices (RCDs): Detect current imbalances and disconnect the circuit.
  • Ground Fault Circuit Interrupters (GFCIs): Similar to RCDs, commonly used in North America.

2.3 Surge Protection

Devices to protect against voltage spikes and transients:

  • Surge Protective Devices (SPDs): Divert excess voltage to ground.
  • Transient Voltage Surge Suppressors (TVSS): Protect sensitive electronic equipment.

2.4 Advanced Protection Technologies

  • Arc Fault Detection Devices (AFDDs): Detect and mitigate electrical arcs that can cause fires.
  • Smart Circuit Breakers: Provide remote monitoring and control capabilities.
  • Adaptive Protection Systems: Adjust protection settings based on real-time system conditions.

3. Earthing

Earthing (or grounding) is a crucial safety measure in electrical systems, providing a safe path for fault currents and helping to stabilize voltage levels.

3.1 Purpose of Earthing

  • Protect people from electric shock
  • Protect equipment from damage due to fault currents
  • Provide a reference point for voltage measurements
  • Dissipate lightning and static charges

3.2 Types of Earthing Systems

  • TT System: Separate earth electrodes for power source and consumer installation.
  • TN System: Power source and consumer earth are combined. Variants include TN-C, TN-S, and TN-C-S.
  • IT System: No direct connection between live parts and earth, used in special installations.

3.3 Earthing Methods

  • Plate Earthing: Using a metal plate buried in the ground.
  • Pipe Earthing: Using a metal pipe driven into the ground.
  • Rod Earthing: Using copper-clad steel rods driven into the ground.
  • Strip or Wire Earthing: Using copper strip or wire buried horizontally.

3.4 Modern Earthing Techniques

  • Chemical Earthing: Uses hygroscopic compounds to improve soil conductivity.
  • Maintenance-Free Earthing: Long-lasting solutions that require minimal upkeep.
  • Smart Grounding Systems: Incorporate monitoring and diagnostic capabilities.

3.5 Earthing in Renewable Energy Systems

Special considerations for solar PV systems, wind turbines, and energy storage systems.

Work Solutions

Problem 1: Cable Selection

A single-phase circuit is to supply a load of 8 kW at 230 V, 50 Hz. The circuit length is 30 meters. Select an appropriate cable size if the voltage drop should not exceed 2.5%.

Solution:

  1. Calculate the current:
    I = P / V = 8000 / 230 = 34.78 A
  2. Calculate the maximum allowable voltage drop:
    Vdrop = 2.5% of 230 V = 0.025 × 230 = 5.75 V
  3. Using the formula: Vdrop = (2 × ρ × L × I) / A
    Where ρ (resistivity of copper) = 1.68 × 10^-8 Ω·m
    Rearranging to solve for A (cross-sectional area):
    A = (2 × ρ × L × I) / Vdrop
    A = (2 × 1.68 × 10^-8 × 30 × 34.78) / 5.75
    A = 6.12 mm²
  4. The next standard size up is 10 mm². This size will ensure the voltage drop is within limits.

Therefore, select a 10 mm² copper cable for this circuit.

Problem 2: RCD Selection

A domestic installation has a main supply rated at 100 A. Calculate the rating of the main RCD if it's required to disconnect within 40 ms for a touch voltage not exceeding 50 V.

Solution:

  1. The maximum earth fault loop impedance (Zs) for a 50 V touch voltage is:
    Zs = 50 V / IΔn
    Where IΔn is the rated residual operating current of the RCD
  2. For a 40 ms disconnection time, we typically use an RCD with IΔn = 30 mA
  3. Check if this meets the requirement:
    Zs = 50 / 0.03 = 1666.67 Ω
  4. This high impedance value means that a 30 mA RCD will provide adequate protection
  5. The RCD must also have a current rating equal to or higher than the main supply:
    Required rating ≥ 100 A

Therefore, select a 100 A, 30 mA RCD for the main protection.

Problem 3: Earthing Calculation

Calculate the resistance of a 3-meter copper rod earth electrode with a diameter of 16 mm, driven vertically into soil with a resistivity of 100 Ω·m.

Solution:

  1. Use the formula for rod electrode resistance:
    R = (ρ / 2πL) × [ln(4L/d) - 1]
    Where:
    R = Resistance of the earth electrode (Ω)
    ρ = Soil resistivity (Ω·m)
    L = Length of the rod (m)
    d = Diameter of the rod (m)
  2. Substituting the values:
    R = (100 / 2π×3) × [ln(4×3/0.016) - 1]
    R = (5.31) × [ln(750) - 1]
    R = (5.31) × [6.62 - 1]
    R = (5.31) × 5.62
    R = 29.84 Ω

The resistance of the earth electrode is approximately 29.84 Ω.

1. Design and Install a Basic Lighting Circuit

Objective: Create a simple lighting circuit with switches and outlets.

Materials needed:

  • Wire (14/2 NM cable)
  • Light switches
  • Electrical boxes
  • Light fixtures
  • Wire nuts
  • Circuit breaker
  • Electrical tape

Steps:

  1. Plan the circuit layout
  2. Install electrical boxes for switches and fixtures
  3. Run cable between boxes
  4. Connect switches and fixtures
  5. Install the circuit breaker in the panel
  6. Test the circuit

2. Build a Simple Ground Fault Circuit Interrupter (GFCI) Tester

Objective: Construct a device to test GFCI outlets.

Materials needed:

  • Plastic enclosure
  • Three-prong plug
  • Push button switch
  • 470 kΩ resistor
  • LED
  • Wire

Steps:

  1. Wire the resistor between the hot and ground prongs of the plug
  2. Add the push button switch in series with the resistor
  3. Connect the LED between hot and neutral for indication
  4. Assemble components in the enclosure
  5. Test on a known good GFCI outlet

Steps:

  1. Plan the smart home layout

  3. Install smart outlets

  6. Create automation routines
