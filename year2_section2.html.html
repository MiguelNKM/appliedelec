Section 2: Transformers

Transformers

Transformers are static electrical devices that transfer electrical energy between two or more circuits through electromagnetic induction. They are crucial components in power distribution systems and various electronic applications.

1. Construction of Transformers

Transformers consist of the following main components:

  • Core: Made of laminated silicon steel to reduce eddy current losses
  • Primary Winding: Receives input power
  • Secondary Winding: Delivers output power
  • Insulation: Separates windings and core
  • Tank and Cooling System: For larger transformers

2. Principles of Operation of Transformers

Transformers operate on the principle of electromagnetic induction:

  1. An alternating current in the primary winding creates a changing magnetic field in the core.
  2. This changing magnetic field induces a voltage in the secondary winding.
  3. The ratio of primary to secondary voltage is equal to the ratio of the number of turns in each winding.

The voltage transformation ratio is given by: Vp / Vs = Np / Ns

3. Losses and Temperature Rise in Transformers

Transformer losses can be categorized into two main types:

  • Core Losses (Iron Losses):
    • Hysteresis Loss: Due to magnetization and demagnetization of the core
    • Eddy Current Loss: Due to induced currents in the core material
  • Copper Losses (I²R Losses): Due to resistance in the windings

These losses lead to temperature rise in the transformer, which must be managed to prevent insulation breakdown and reduce efficiency.

4. Efficiency of Transformers

Transformer efficiency is the ratio of output power to input power:

η = (Output Power / Input Power) × 100%

Efficiency can be improved by:

  • Using high-quality core materials
  • Optimizing winding design
  • Improving cooling systems

5. Cooling of Transformers

Cooling methods for transformers include:

  • Oil Natural Air Natural (ONAN)
  • Oil Natural Air Forced (ONAF)
  • Oil Forced Air Forced (OFAF)
  • Oil Directed Air Forced (ODAF)

The choice of cooling method depends on the transformer's size, power rating, and operating conditions.

6. Voltage Regulation

Voltage regulation in transformers is the change in secondary voltage from no-load to full-load conditions, expressed as a percentage of the full-load voltage:

% Regulation = ((Vnl - Vfl) / Vfl) × 100%

Where Vnl is the no-load voltage and Vfl is the full-load voltage.

Factors affecting voltage regulation include:

  • Winding resistance
  • Leakage reactance
  • Load power factor

Work Solutions

Problem 1: Transformer Turns Ratio and Voltage

A transformer has 500 turns in its primary winding and 100 turns in its secondary winding. If the primary voltage is 230V, calculate:

  1. The turns ratio
  2. The secondary voltage

Solution:

  1. Turns ratio:

    a = Np / Ns = 500 / 100 = 5:1

  2. Secondary voltage:

    Vp / Vs = Np / Ns

    230 / Vs = 500 / 100

    Vs = 230 × (100 / 500) = 46V

Problem 2: Transformer Efficiency

A 50 kVA transformer has iron losses of 300W and full-load copper losses of 700W. Calculate the efficiency of the transformer at:

  1. Full load, 0.8 power factor lagging
  2. Half load, 0.8 power factor lagging

Solution:

  1. Full load efficiency:

    Output power = 50 × 0.8 = 40 kW

    Input power = Output power + Iron losses + Copper losses

    Input power = 40000 + 300 + 700 = 41000W

    Efficiency = (Output power / Input power) × 100%

    Efficiency = (40000 / 41000) × 100% = 97.56%

  2. Half load efficiency:

    Output power = 25 × 0.8 = 20 kW

    Copper losses at half load = 700 × (0.5)2 = 175W

    Input power = 20000 + 300 + 175 = 20475W

    Efficiency = (20000 / 20475) × 100% = 97.68%
